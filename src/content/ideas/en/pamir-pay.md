Fewer than one in five Afghans had a bank account. Khalid and Tamana believed
a phone number should be enough. Pamir Pay let shopkeepers accept payments
and families receive remittances with nothing but a feature phone and a
four-digit code.

They spent eighteen months navigating central bank licensing, signed an
agency agreement with a telecom operator, and ran a 2,000-user pilot in
Kabul's Kote Sangi district. Merchants loved it: no cash to guard, no
counterfeit notes.

In 2021 the regulatory framework they had built on dissolved, correspondent
banking relationships evaporated under sanctions, and their compliance
officer, then their CTO, then their CEO left the country. The company was
wound down before the product ever launched publicly.

The pilot's final report — user research, agent economics, fraud playbooks —
was donated to a university library. Whoever builds Afghan mobile money next
will start eighteen months ahead.
