Aseel Express started with a simple observation: Kabul's shopkeepers lost
customers every day because there was no reliable way to get goods across the
city. Omid and Freshta began with three motorbikes, a WhatsApp number, and a
paper ledger.

By 2020 they had forty riders, an Android app for order tracking, and
partnerships with two supermarket chains. Deliveries crossed 300 a day during
Ramadan. Investors from the diaspora were in due-diligence for a seed round.

Then August 2021 came. The banking system froze, and international transfers —
the lifeline of their fuel-and-salary float — became impossible. Half the team
left the country within months. The riders kept going for a while, paid in
cash from the founders' savings, but by December the savings were gone.

Aseel Express stopped. But forty families learned what a logistics business
looks like from the inside, the routing spreadsheets still circulate among
Kabul delivery startups, and two former riders now run their own three-bike
courier services.

Every attempt is a step.
