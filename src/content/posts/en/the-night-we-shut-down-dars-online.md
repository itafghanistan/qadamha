People imagine shutting down a startup as a meeting, a decision, a
spreadsheet. For me it was a Thursday night, alone with a laptop, hovering
over a button that said *Terminate instances*.

Four years of video lessons lived on those servers. I knew the mirrors
existed — the volunteers had copied everything to drives in three cities —
but it still felt like turning off a light in a room where children were
reading.

I pressed it. The invoice stopped growing. And the next morning a teacher
from Ghor called to ask why the site was down, and then said something I
will keep forever: "Send me the files. We'll manage without the internet.
We always have."

That's when I understood the project hadn't died. It had just changed shape
— from a website into ten thousand memory cards. If you're building
education tech in Afghanistan today: design for the memory card first. The
internet is a luxury; the hunger to learn is not.
